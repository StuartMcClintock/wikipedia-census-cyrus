'''Stafford Springs''' is a [[census-designated place]] located in [[Stafford, Connecticut]], United States. The population was 4,780 at the [[2020 United States Census|2020 Census]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://data.census.gov/profile/Stafford_Springs_CDP;_Connecticut?g=160XX00US0972300|title=Stafford Springs CDP, Connecticut|publisher=[[United States Census Bureau]]|language=en|access-date=May 13, 2023}}</ref> The village was a [[Borough (Connecticut)|borough]] until November 1991, when it was disincorporated.<ref>{{Cite book|title=1992 Census of Wholesale Trade: Geographic Area Series, Connecticut|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=0Bbjf6dxVB8C&pg=SL4-PA9|publisher=U.S. Census Bureau|page=Appendix F: Geographic Notes|year=1994}}</ref>

Located near the source of the [[Willimantic River]],<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://connecticuthistory.org/towns-page/stafford/|title=Stafford|website=connecticuthistory.org|date=28 October 2011 |publisher=Connecticut Humanities|access-date=2016-11-25}}</ref>  the mill industry grew in the town due to its location and became the largest industry in the area.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.staffordct.org/history.php#his|title=About Stafford, Connecticut: History|publisher=Town of Stafford|access-date=2016-11-25}}</ref> The village has the [[Holt Memorial Fountain]] and the former railroad station.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://ctmonuments.net/2013/04/holt-memorial-fountain-stafford-springs/|title=Holt Memorial Fountain, Stafford Springs|website=CTMonuments.net|access-date=2016-11-25}}</ref> A portion of the village center was listed on the [[National Register of Historic Places]] in 2025.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nps.gov/subjects/nationalregister/weekly-list-2025-04-18.htm|title=WEEKLY LIST OF ACTIONS TAKEN ON PROPERTIES: 4/11/2025 THROUGH 4/18/2025|publisher=National Park Service|accessdate=2025-04-25}}</ref>

In the 18th century, the [[Spring (hydrology)|spring]] at Stafford Springs was famous for its reputed ability to cure "the [[gout]], sterility, pulmonary, hysterics, etc."<ref name="Chapelle">Frank Chapelle, ''Wellsprings: A Natural History of Bottled Spring Waters'' (Rutgers University Press, 2005), pp. 107-08.</ref> In 1771, [[John Adams]], then a young lawyer, visited Stafford Springs for several days after suffering from overwork and anxiety.<ref name="Chapelle"/>

Stafford Springs was once the headquarters of Station C of the [[Connecticut State Police]], and subsequently was the site of the Troop C Barracks.<ref>Jerry Longo, ''Images of America: Connecticut State Police'' (Arcadia Publishing: 2003), p. 2.</ref>

Currently [[Connecticut Route 32|Route 32]], [[Connecticut Route 190|Route 190]], [[Connecticut Route 19|Route 19]], and [[Connecticut Route 140|Route 140]] pass through or originate in the village.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.ct.gov/dot/lib/dot/documents/dpolicy/hwylog/hwylog.pdf|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20050518014140/http://ct.gov/dot/LIB/dot/Documents/dpolicy/hwylog/hwylog.pdf|url-status=dead|archive-date=May 18, 2005|title=Highway Log Connecticut State Numbered Routes and Roads|date=December 31, 2014|website=ct.gov/dot|publisher=Connecticut Department of Transportation|access-date=November 27, 2016}}</ref>

== Geography ==
According to the [[United States Census Bureau]], the CDP has a total area of 6.41&nbsp;mi<sup>2</sup> (16.6&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>), of which  6.37&nbsp;mi<sup>2</sup> (16.5&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>)  is land and 0.036&nbsp;mi<sup>2</sup> (0.093&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>)  (0.56%) is water.<ref name="GR1">{{cite web|url=https://www.census.gov/geographies/reference-files/time-series/geo/gazetteer-files.html|publisher=[[United States Census Bureau]]|accessdate=2020-11-07|title=US Gazetteer files: 2019}}</ref>
